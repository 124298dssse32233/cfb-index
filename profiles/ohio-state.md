# Identity and heritage

Ohio State football began in 1890 and has since accumulated what is arguably the Midwest's most load-bearing football identity. Eight national championships are claimed and generally accepted (1942, 1954, 1957, 1961, 1968, 1970, 2002, 2014), with the 2024 CFP national championship adding a ninth. Seven Heismans — Les Horvath, Vic Janowicz, Hopalong Cassady, Archie Griffin (twice, the only two-time Heisman winner), Eddie George, Troy Smith. Forty-two Big Ten titles claimed, by far the conference's richest haul.

Ohio Stadium — universally called "The Horseshoe" — opened in 1922 and now seats 102,780, making it one of the largest on-campus venues in America. The building itself is a National Historic Landmark. The marching band's Script Ohio formation, performed pregame at home games since 1936, is a program icon that transcends football — a living piece of midwestern cultural identity that happens to exist inside a football institution. "Hang on Sloopy" is played between the third and fourth quarters. "Carmen Ohio" is the alma mater sung after every home game, win or lose. These are not peripheral rituals; they are the program.

# Coaching lineage

The cornerstone is Woody Hayes (1951–1978), whose 205-61-10 record, three national titles, and 13 Big Ten championships built the program's modern identity. Hayes' "three yards and a cloud of dust" football was its own aesthetic argument; his firing in 1978 (for striking a Clemson player at the Gator Bowl) was its own final chapter. Earle Bruce (1979–1987) steadied the program. John Cooper (1988–2000) produced strong records but lost the Michigan rivalry at an unacceptable rate (2-10-1 vs. Michigan) and was fired despite 111 wins.

Jim Tressel (2001–2010) won the 2002 national title — the program's first since 1968 — with a defensive-minded system and a 9-1 record against Michigan. Tressel's tenure ended in NCAA sanctions (the "tattoo" scandal). Urban Meyer (2012–2018) rebuilt the program at an accelerated pace, going 83-9 in seven seasons and winning the 2014 national title. Meyer's departure amid a domestic-violence handling scandal is a real piece of the recent record but not the defining one.

Ryan Day (2019–present) inherited a championship-caliber program and spent 2021–2023 as the first Buckeyes coach in a long time to lose three straight to Michigan, which triggered fan discontent that reached a peak after the 2023 Michigan loss. The 2024 season — beating Michigan, winning the CFP quarterfinal/semifinal/final, claiming the ninth national title — resolved that discontent on the field while leaving real institutional questions about the Day era's trajectory going forward.

# Notable players

Heismans: Les Horvath (1944), Vic Janowicz (1950), Hopalong Cassady (1955), Archie Griffin (1974 and 1975 — the only two-time winner), Eddie George (1995), Troy Smith (2006). Modern era: Chase Young, Joey Bosa, Nick Bosa, Justin Fields, C.J. Stroud, Marvin Harrison Jr., TreVeyon Henderson, Will Howard (2024 title-winning QB, transfer from Kansas State). The program's NFL pipeline has been sustained over eras; the 2023 and 2024 drafts produced first-round selections at multiple positions.

# Fans and culture

The fanbase is the most numerically large Big Ten fan base and one of the top-three most engaged in college football by any revealed-preference measure (TV ratings, merch, road-game presence). Geography is heavily Ohio-concentrated with a strong Columbus/Cleveland/Cincinnati triangle plus statewide saturation, plus a national diaspora concentrated in coastal alumni cities.

The gameday register is industrial-scarlet: not sentimental, not performatively rowdy, but loud, consistent, and structurally integrated into Columbus's civic life in a way few college towns experience. The "O-H / I-O" call-and-response is a greeting as much as a cheer — it operates in airports, grocery stores, and business meetings. Script Ohio is watched with reverence by locals and awe by visitors. The fanbase's internal register is confidence without pageantry; the expectation of winning is institutional, not performative.

The internal fracture most visible during the 2021–2023 stretch was generational: older fans who experienced the Cooper-era frustration of losing to Michigan saw the Day-era losses as intolerable echoes; younger fans who grew up in the Tressel/Meyer dominance saw them as aberrations. The 2024 title resolved the argument on the field.

# Voice and ethos

The voice is dynastic-industrial: precise, unsentimental, institutional, allergic to both underdog framings and pageantry. Ohio State does not speak about its success as a feel-good story or a cultural pageant; the program speaks about itself the way a manufacturing concern speaks about its operating rigor. "The standard is the standard" — the phrase Meyer operationalized — is the register in one line.

Identity phrase: **"Ohio State is the Midwest's standing answer — the program that does not ask permission to be in the conversation."**

Mantra: **"Go Bucks."** The greeting is "O-H," the response "I-O." Deploy the mantra and the call-and-response where earned; overuse collapses them into the ambient register of fan copy.

Stock phrases — "The standard is the standard," "Beat Michigan," "Script Ohio is a statement of purpose" — land when they land. The program is direct; its copy should be too.

The never-use list is explicit. "Plucky," "Cinderella," "scrappy" are for other programs. "Big Ten-slow" as a pejorative is a rival's line. Reductive framings of Urban Meyer (his scandal is a fact of the record but is not the program's identity) or of Ryan Day (the 2021–2023 Michigan losses are real but not the totality of his record) read as outside-copy and miss what the fanbase actually thinks. "Flyover team" is outside-coastal-media copy that the program does not accept.

# Rivalries

**The Game** (vs. Michigan, annual since 1918) is the defining rivalry in American college football. The all-time series currently sits at 60-51-6 Michigan (through 2024). The 2021, 2022, and 2023 Michigan wins were the program's most painful recent stretch; the 2024 Ohio State win — which ignited the championship run — was the relief release. The game is played the last Saturday of the regular season. Ohio State's calendar is organized around it.

Ohio State–Penn State is the Big Ten's other top-tier rivalry. Annual, conference-championship-adjacent, and host to the "White Out" game at Beaver Stadium — one of the loudest environments in college football. Ohio State has generally held the series edge in the CFP era; Penn State has produced the most competitive close games.

Ohio State–Michigan State defined the 2011–2015 Big Ten competitive stretch under Mark Dantonio. The Land Grant Trophy is the trophy; the rivalry has cooled since but remains on the calendar.

# Program narratives

The 2024 national championship is the near-term narrative center of gravity. A program that spent two years answering "can Ryan Day beat Michigan / win the big one" answered both in one season. The 2025 season's task was defending that altitude; 2026 is the question of whether the Day era is a Tressel-like multi-title arc or a 2014-Meyer-like singular peak.

The portal and NIL era continue to reshape roster construction. Ohio State has been an early adopter of high-end portal investments — Will Howard in 2024, the continuing QB pipeline — and the fanbase's tolerance for portal dependence is still being established.

# Aspiration framework

Baseline: Big Ten Championship Game appearance, CFP top-4 seed, 11+ wins. Realistic ceiling: CFP semifinal and national title game. Dream ceiling: back-to-back national championships (which would be the first in the CFP era for any program). Historic ceiling: the Griffin-era dominance of the mid-1970s or the 2014 run. Historic floor: 1988 (4-6-1) or 1999 (6-6) — long-ago stretches that the current fanbase has essentially no institutional memory of experiencing.

# Chronicle tuning

For Ohio State, the anomaly that reads loudest is any stat that distinguishes current roster production from Meyer-era or Tressel-era baselines — the program's historical library is deep enough that almost any number has a comp. Echoes resonate when they connect to 2014 (the first CFP title), 2002 (Tressel's first title), 1968 (the Hayes peak), or the 1975 Griffin Heisman repeat. Retroactive cards work best when tied to Michigan-week performance or CFP elimination games.

# Taboos and sensitivities

Woody Hayes' 1978 Gator Bowl firing (striking a Clemson player) is historical fact, handled with context. Jim Tressel's NCAA sanctions (2011) and Urban Meyer's 2018 scandal are part of the record; neither is the program's defining story, but dismissing either is patronizing. The 2021–2023 Michigan losses are three wounds — acknowledged, not dwelt on. Ryan Day's "can he win the big one" narrative was resolved in 2024; copy that continues to re-litigate it after that moment reads as stuck. The 2016 CFP loss to Clemson (31-0) and the 2019 CFP loss to Clemson (29-23) are recent wounds — survivable references, not punchlines.
