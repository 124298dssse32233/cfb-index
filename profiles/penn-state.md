# Identity and heritage

Penn State football was founded in 1887 and played as an independent for most of the 20th century before joining the Big Ten for football in 1993. Two claimed national championships: 1982 and 1986, both under Joe Paterno. One Heisman (John Cappelletti, 1973). Seven undefeated seasons, most recent 1994 (11-0, controversial-not-selected-for-title-game due to polling politics). Four Big Ten championships.

Beaver Stadium — 106,572 capacity, second-largest in the Big Ten behind Michigan's Big House — opened in 1960 and has been expanded multiple times. The White Out game (student section coordinated white t-shirts, expanded to the whole stadium, typically the highest-profile home game) is annually one of college football's loudest environments — ESPN analysts routinely rank it the loudest in the sport.

"Success with Honor" is the program's self-articulated ethos — a phrase Paterno deployed and that survived into the post-Paterno era. "We Are" / "Penn State" is the universal call-and-response that predates specific uses.

The 2011–2012 Sandusky scandal and its aftermath — the firing of Paterno, the NCAA sanctions (since partially reversed), the vacating and restoration of records, the civil and institutional litigation — is a real piece of the program's record. The program has rebuilt through and past the scandal under coaches Bill O'Brien, James Franklin; the current identity emphasizes the program's continuity rather than the scandal's interruption.

# Coaching lineage

Hugo Bezdek (1918–1929) and Bob Higgins (1930–1948) anchored the pre-war program. Rip Engle (1950–1965) hired Joe Paterno as his assistant. Joe Paterno (1966–2011) is the program's central figure — 409 wins (most in FBS history at the time of his firing; later vacated and largely restored), two national titles, five undefeated seasons, the program's public face for 46 years. Paterno's firing in November 2011 amid the Sandusky grand jury indictment ended his tenure abruptly; he died in January 2012.

Bill O'Brien (2012–2013) took the program through the sanctions' first years, produced an 8-4 first season under unprecedented scholarship restrictions, then left for the Houston Texans. James Franklin (2014–present) is the longest-tenured post-Paterno coach. Record to date: 90-38, four New Year's Six bowls, one Big Ten title (2016), one CFP appearance (2016 — lost in Rose Bowl to USC). The Franklin era has produced strong regular-season records without breaking through to a national title-game appearance, the program's central 2020s narrative.

# Fans and culture

The fanbase is central-Pennsylvania-concentrated with a national alumni diaspora (particularly in the Northeast corridor and the DMV / NC area). The gameday register is blue-collar-dynastic: serious, coordinated, engaged, with a specific Penn State character that is more Pennsylvania Rust Belt than elite-Midwest-academic. The White Out game is the fanbase's peak expression — 106,000 fans in coordinated white, "Zombie Nation" blaring between plays, the cumulative noise levels approaching the pain threshold.

"We Are" / "Penn State" is the continuous call-and-response — the call appears organically from any fan in any location; the response follows from any other fan. Ritual: the alma mater sung post-game, the helmet's plain white stripe-no-logo aesthetic (PSU is one of the few programs without a logo on the helmet), the blue-and-white color identity, the Nittany Lion shrine on campus.

The internal fracture most visible: the Paterno-era fans who hold onto Paterno's legacy (including his handling of the Sandusky investigation) as institutional memory, and the post-Paterno fans who've built a program identity separate from him. This fracture remains active; the Paterno statue was removed from outside Beaver Stadium in 2012 and has not returned.

The voice is blue-collar-dynastic: earnest, Pennsylvania-specific, uninterested in coastal pageantry or Hollywood register, confident about the program's standard without performative swagger. Penn State speaks about itself the way a well-run mid-sized manufacturing company speaks — confident in the work, measured in the self-promotion.

# Rivalries

**Penn State–Ohio State** has been the program's modern identity rivalry since joining the Big Ten in 1993. Annual. The 2016 Penn State 24-21 win (blocked field goal returned for TD) was Franklin's breakthrough moment. The 2022 Penn State loss and 2023 Ohio State White Out game are recent markers. The series heavily favors Ohio State historically, but the margin narrows in close games.

**Penn State–Michigan** emerged post-1993. The 2016 overtime win and the 2022 matchup are canonical. Michigan–PSU is now the program's second Big Ten rivalry by intensity.

**Penn State–Pittsburgh** is the older in-state rivalry, played intermittently in recent decades. Pitt alumni consider it the rivalry; PSU alumni generally do not.

**Penn State–Michigan State** (Land Grant Trophy) is the annual Big Ten crossover with shared agricultural-university identity. Less intense than the others.

# Program narratives

The Franklin-era "can he win the big one" question is the program's central open narrative. Four New Year's Six bowls, one Big Ten title (2016), no CFP semifinal appearance, two fourth-quarter collapses vs. Ohio State (2022, 2024) that fanbase patience has not processed past. The 2026 season is the program's 13th Franklin year — the question of whether the ceiling is permanent or breaks is the annual refrain.

The portal and NIL era has been mixed for Penn State — strong recruiting of in-state and northeast high school talent, less dominant in portal big-ticket competition with SEC peers. Drew Allar's 2024 Heisman campaign and the 2024 CFP appearance (11-3, semifinal loss) were the Franklin era's high-water mark.
