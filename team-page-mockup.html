What Moved the Model

A great team page should translate the math into plain English so users understand why a score mattered.
Largest Climb: +2.7 vs Georgia

The team beat an elite opponent with real defensive control. That kind of win changes national belief much more than routing a weak team by thirty.

Quiet Win: +0.2 vs Eastern Illinois

Comfortable, but close to expectation. The model treats “you should have won” as maintenance, not a headline.

Biggest Hit: -2.1 at Tennessee

Not because the team lost, but because it looked less stable than its rating. The combination of opponent quality, margin, and prior form matters more than the `L` alone.

Why This Direction Fits Your Data

Build Around What You Can Guarantee

SportsDB is strong on schedules, identities, seasons, and artwork. It is uneven on college football timelines, complete rosters, and universal deep event stats. So the mockup leans into exactly the layer you control: ratings, movement, context, and history.

Better Than a Stats Dump
The schedule becomes the backbone.

Every game already exists as a strong story object: opponent, date, result, score, location, season, round, and your rating delta.

Your model creates the page’s proprietary value.

Pregame and postgame ratings, opponent quality at game time, volatility, and resume logic are what make the page feel like your product rather than an API wrapper.

The page can scale across all levels.

This exact structure works for FBS, FCS, D2, or D3 because it depends on games and derived ratings, not on perfect play-by-play coverage.

Mockup notes: this page intentionally favors rating movement, historical arc, and schedule-first storytelling because those are the strongest surfaces you can build confidently on top of SportsDB plus your own power model. All numbers and deltas here are illustrative.
