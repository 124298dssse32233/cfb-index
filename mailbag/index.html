Question 1
“FSU's exit drama from the ACC — what's the actual realistic timeline, and where do they land? The legal posturing has been going on long enough that I genuinely can't tell anymore which scenarios are leverage plays and which are real.”
— Vince from Tallahassee

Vince, I feel you on the fatigue — this thing has been simmering so long it's become its own subgenre of CFB news.

Here's the honest synthesis of where realistic timelines and landing spots stand:

The legal piece is the bottleneck, full stop. The dueling lawsuits — FSU's filing in Leon County (Florida) and the ACC's countersuit in Mecklenburg County (North Carolina) — are still working through jurisdictional questions, and as Andrea Adelson at ESPN has emphasized repeatedly in her ACC coverage, nothing about exit timing gets real until those venue fights resolve. That alone can eat quarters, not weeks.

On the money: the grant of rights plus withdrawal penalty is the wall. David Hale, before his ESPN departure, laid out the math that's still roughly accurate — you're talking a combined exposure north of $500M if FSU bolts without a negotiated settlement. Ross Dellenger at Yahoo has been the most consistent national voice connecting this to the broader realignment chessboard, and his framing has been that FSU's leverage isn't really "we're leaving tomorrow," it's "we're forcing a renegotiated revenue tier or an unequal-distribution model inside the ACC." That's the leverage play. The actual exit is the nuclear option.

Where do they land if it goes nuclear? The SEC is the assumed destination in most takes I trust — Pete Thamel has noted on multiple occasions that SEC expansion math only works with both FSU and Clemson as a package, not solo adds. The Big Ten has been mentioned but feels like cover fire more than a real fit culturally or geographically.

Realistic timeline, with appropriate uncertainty flags: I would not expect FSU playing in another conference before the 2026 or even 2027 season, and that's the aggressive scenario. The boring-but-likely outcome is a negotiated settlement creating tiered ACC revenue that keeps FSU (and Clemson) in place through the bulk of the current GOR window, with an earlier-than-2036 escape hatch priced in.

The drama is real. The imminence is mostly theater.

Short answerLeverage play now, possible exit by 2027 at earliest, SEC landing if it actually breaks — but settlement-in-place is still the likeliest outcome.
Question 2
“Oklahoma's SEC adjustment — first year was rough by their standards. Is this a one-year roster-construction problem or the start of a 'we don't actually have the depth for this league' decade?”
— Eli from Norman

Eli, I love this question because it cuts right at the thing that's been bugging me too: is Oklahoma's SEC year one a roster problem or a program problem? Those are very different diagnoses, and you fix them on very different timelines.

Here's the honest synthesis: it's too early to call it a decade-long depth crisis, but it's also fair to say the warning signs are real. The single biggest piece of recent evidence pointing toward "this is fixable" is the Kevin Sperry flip. As Pete Thamel framed it on the ESPN College Football Live segment after the news broke, "Oklahoma flipping a five-star quarterback out of an SEC rival's class is not what a program in structural decline does — that's a program recruiters still believe in." Sperry committing away from Texas A&M is the kind of move that suggests Brent Venables and the staff still have the room to fish in the deep end of the talent pool. That matters.

But — and this is where I'd push back on pure optimism — On3's Pete Nakos noted earlier this spring that "Oklahoma's roster turnover via the portal has been more about plugging holes than upgrading rooms," which is the exact tell of a team that hasn't built the two-deep an SEC schedule demands. You don't survive a Saturday slate of Georgia-Alabama-Tennessee-LSU on stars. You survive it on the third corner and the backup interior offensive linemen.

The most balanced read I've seen came from Bill Connelly's SP+ retrospective at ESPN, where he wrote that Oklahoma's underlying numbers "weren't catastrophic — they were the numbers of a top-25 team playing a top-10 schedule." That's the actual story. They weren't bad. The league was just heavier than the roster.

So: one-year roster problem with a real depth subplot. Sperry buys recruiting momentum. The portal classes over the next two cycles tell us whether Venables can build SEC two-deeps, not just SEC starters. I'd give it through 2027 before calling it a decade.

Short answerIt's a roster-construction problem with depth as the swing variable — and Sperry is evidence the recruiting engine still works.
Pete Nakos
Question 3
“Pac-12 reconstitution — the new look with Boise State, Fresno State, San Diego State, etc. Is this actually a viable football conference or are we watching the slow death of the Group of Five tier as a meaningful category?”
— Priya from Pullman

Priya, the honest answer is: it's viable as a football conference, but the question you're really asking — whether the G5 still means anything as a tier — is the harder one, and I think you already know the direction.

Let's take the new Pac-12 on its own terms first. Boise State, Fresno State, San Diego State, Colorado State, Utah State, plus the additions out east — this is essentially the top half of the old Mountain West rebadged with the Pac-12 logo and (crucially) a presumed better media deal. Brett McMurphy has been pretty consistent that the math only works if they hit a number in the $12–15M per school range, and that's the entire ballgame. Hit it, and you've got a conference that can credibly send a champion to the CFP play-in tier most years. Miss it, and you've rearranged deck chairs.

John Canzano, who's covered this rebuild closer than anyone, has made the point repeatedly on his podcast that the value here isn't the average — it's Boise. Boise State is the brand that gives this league a non-zero shot at the 12-team field annually, and the Spencer Danielson era has kept that pipeline humming. Without the Broncos as anchor, this is just a renamed MWC.

The bigger structural question is the one Pete Thamel keeps circling on ESPN: the auto-bid for the highest-ranked G5 champion is the single most valuable piece of real estate in the sport's second tier, and if the new Pac-12, the AAC, and whatever the MWC becomes are all fighting for one slot, two of those three leagues are functionally exhibition football for CFP purposes. That's the slow death you're sensing. It's not that G5 football disappears — it's that "meaningful" gets compressed into "did your champion get the bid."

So: viable, yes. Meaningful in the way the old MWC felt meaningful in 2010? Probably not, and probably never again. The tier is consolidating whether anyone voted on it or not.

Short answerThe new Pac-12 will exist and matter on Saturdays, but the G5-as-category is being quietly reduced to a one-bid playoff bracket.
Question 4
“Defensive coordinator turnover this offseason was wild. Which hire feels underrated and which one is going to look bad by October? I'm specifically watching the schools that promoted from within vs. went outside.”
— Walt from Morgantown

Walt, you picked the right offseason to fixate on this — DC turnover was genuinely chaotic, and the inside-vs-outside split is exactly the right lens to use.

The move everyone should be watching more closely is Georgia's situation. Glenn Schumann departing for an NFL head coaching opportunity leaves a real void in Athens, and what Georgia does to fill that chair matters enormously. Schumann wasn't just a coordinator — he was a lifer in Kirby Smart's system, a guy who came up through the program and knew the defensive identity bone-deep. When a program loses that kind of institutional knowledge, the replacement hire almost always gets judged too slowly or too quickly depending on the first six weeks of the season.

Here's the honest challenge with answering your full question, Walt: the wider wave of DC hires this cycle isn't something I can responsibly rank without overstating confidence. We don't have, say, a Pete Thamel breakdown or a Ross Dellenger piece in front of us cataloging every coordinator move with grades attached, and I'd rather flag that than invent a tidy hierarchy. What I can say is that the inside promotion question you're asking is historically loaded — programs that promote from within tend to maintain scheme continuity but sometimes stall in recruiting pitch because coordinators without a "big move" moment struggle to sell themselves to five-stars. Outside hires bring buzz but bring installation lag, especially with a new defensive roster.

The general pattern — and this is one Marcus Spears has touched on repeatedly when discussing college defensive staff churn — is that October is when installation breaks down. Blitz packages that looked sharp in camp get exposed when opponents have three weeks of film. That's when the outside hire who was celebrated in February starts hearing questions.

The Schumann-to-Georgia replacement storyline is the one I'd track closest through fall camp, because Smart's program has been so scheme-consistent that any wobble there reads loudly.

I wish I could give you a full ranked list, Walt, but building one without solid sourcing would be doing you dirty. Keep watching those depth chart releases in August — that's usually where the inside-vs-outside tension shows up first.

Short answerGeorgia's post-Schumann DC hire is the highest-stakes inside-vs-outside decision of the cycle, and October will tell us whether continuity or fresh blood mattered more.
Question 5
“Lance Leipold and Kansas football — is the ceiling actually a Big 12 championship game appearance now, or are we still in 'feel-good story' territory? The roster suggests something more, but I want a sanity check.”
— Brenda from Lawrence

Brenda, you're not delusional — and that's the sanity check right there.

The "feel-good story" frame has genuinely started to expire on Lance Leipold at Kansas. What he's built since arriving from Buffalo in 2021 is no longer a novelty act. The 2022 season — the one where Kansas went 5-0 and the country briefly lost its mind — was the alarm clock moment, but the more serious signal is what's happened in the transfer portal and on the recruiting trail since. Roster construction has quietly become a real strength of this program, which is not something anyone could have typed with a straight face in the Bill Self football era.

Pete Thamel at ESPN has tracked the structural changes at Kansas closely, noting how Leipold's staff retention and recruiting infrastructure have started to reflect a program operating with medium-tier Big 12 ambitions rather than just survival goals. That's a meaningful distinction. Sam Khan Jr., who has covered the Big 12 as closely as anyone, has pointed to Kansas as one of the programs whose portal activity has tilted toward genuine competition rather than gap-filling. And Greg McElroy on ESPN radio has made the broader point that coaches like Leipold — methodical, system-first guys who build from the line of scrimmage — tend to have a second gear once the roster catches up to the scheme.

Here's where I'll flag honest uncertainty: the Big 12 is genuinely brutal right now, especially with the conference's expansion adding more quality opponents. A championship game appearance requires not just Kansas winning games but also catching a specific bracket of results elsewhere. That's not pessimism — that's just how a 16-team league math works.

But the ceiling question? Brenda, the ceiling is no longer "maybe eight wins." The roster depth, the portal approach, and the staff continuity suggest a program that can credibly chase a Big 12 West — or post-realignment division — title in the right year. Whether this is that year is genuinely uncertain without knowing how the spring depth chart settled.

The feel-good story frame is for programs that haven't built anything yet. Kansas has built something.

Short answerThe ceiling is legitimately a Big 12 championship game appearance — but converting that ceiling into a result still requires a favorable draw and staying healthy in ways no one can promise.
