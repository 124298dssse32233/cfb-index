Spring 2026: The Series Holds, the Programs Diverge Again, and the Rivalry's Future Is the Schedule's Discipline

Two cycles of post-realignment ND-USC are in the books. The series is intact. The future depends on whether USC and Notre Dame keep deciding it should be intact.

Spring of 2026 finds the ND-USC series in the same place every elite cross-regional rivalry eventually finds itself: structurally durable but contingently scheduled, dependent on the two programs' continued institutional commitment to keep the matchup on the calendar each year. The 2025 season closed with Notre Dame finishing 11-2 and missing the CFP semifinal in a quarterfinal loss to Texas; USC finished 9-4 and made the bracket as the No. 11 seed, where the program lost in the first round to Tennessee at home. The asymmetry that we wrote up in Chapter 2 has compressed but has not closed. USC is closer to peer status than the Trojan program of November 2024 was. Notre Dame is structurally elevated relative to where the program had been before Marcus Freeman's title-game run. The series is being played by two programs whose competitive levels are different but no longer dramatically so.

Marcus Freeman, in the third year of the head-coaching run that we have tracked across these chapters, has now produced two consecutive seasons of CFP-level work. The 2024 season ended in Atlanta. The 2025 season ended in the quarterfinals to Texas, in a game that Notre Dame led at the half and lost in a fourth quarter that Texas's offensive front controlled. The two seasons together — and we noted in Chapter 2 that GoldRush89 had written that the program was "not who we were a year ago" — have established the program's institutional ceiling at a level South Bend has not consistently operated at since the Lou Holtz era. "We are now a program whose ceiling is the College Football Playoff every year," one BlueGrayGold reply in a January 2026 thread read. "That is a different program than the one we have been since 1993. The work to operate at that ceiling is the work of every offseason from now on. Our time is now is no longer aspirational. It is the actual standard."

“The series is older than any of us. The series will outlast all of us. Standards inherited.”

— Antonio Morales, The Athletic USC March column

USC's spring, parallel to Notre Dame's, has been the chapter Antonio Morales has been writing in installments for two years. "USC has done the work," he wrote in his March 2026 column. "The defense under Lynn is now structurally credible. The offense under Riley is, as it always has been, elite. The recruiting class for 2026 finished No. 9 nationally — the program's best class since 2018. The program is no longer in the conversation about whether Riley's hire is producing returns. The program is in the conversation about whether the returns will produce a Big Ten title and a CFP semifinal, which is the standard the program has been operating to since the moment it joined the Big Ten."

What this does to the November 2026 meeting — which is in South Bend, on the regularly-scheduled date, with both programs entering as preseason top-fifteen teams — is that it produces the chapter ND-USC has not yet had in the post-realignment era: the meeting between two peer-quality programs, both of whom have legitimate playoff stakes, both of whom carry the institutional weight the rivalry has historically carried. We noted in Chapter 1 that ND-USC was the only rivalry in college football in which both programs measured themselves against the rivalry itself, not against the rest of the schedule. The 2026 meeting will be the first one since 2017 in which both programs are also being measured against the broader playoff race. That is a different kind of stakes. That is the stakes the rivalry has been wanting to find again.

The schedule's structural future is the chapter that does not write itself. The contractual situation we flagged in Chapter 3 — that USC's 2030 contract cycle is the next moment when the Notre Dame slot could come under structural pressure — has not changed. What has changed is the institutional mood inside USC's athletic department. Jen Cohen, in a March interview with Pete Sampson at The Athletic that received unusual amplification in the rivalry's beat coverage, said: "Notre Dame is on our schedule because Notre Dame should be on our schedule. The conversations we have about non-conference scheduling start with Notre Dame and then move outward. That is the program's institutional position. That position is not currently under review." The line read as a deliberate institutional reassertion. The boards on WeAreSC processed it as such. "The series is safe through this contract cycle," one reply read. "The next cycle starts in 2030. The conversations about that cycle start in 2028. We have time. The program's leadership is on the right side of this question. The program's fans are on the right side of this question. As long as both stay on the right side, the series stays on the schedule."

The boards on BlueGrayGold, in a March 2026 thread by GoldRush89 titled "Where the rivalry stands", ran 320 replies and the dominant tone was the institutional patience that the program's voice has carried through the thread. "The series has had asymmetric moments before," one reply read. "It had them in the late 1990s when USC was rebuilding from the post-Smith era. It had them in the Charlie Weis years when Notre Dame was rebuilding from itself. The asymmetric moments do not last. The series is older than any of us. The series will outlast all of us. Standards inherited. We will be there in November 2026. We will be there in November 2030. The schedule holds."

What this thread is going to do, going forward, is treat the rivalry as the institutional case study it has always been: the rivalry that two programs choose to keep playing because the rivalry's institutional value is greater than any single season's competitive logic. ND-USC has been an institutional choice for ninety-eight years. The post-realignment chapters have tested whether the choice would survive in its modern form. Two cycles of testing have produced the answer: yes, structurally, the choice survives. The schedule is the discipline. The programs' continued mutual commitment is the practice of the discipline.

We pick this thread up after the 2026 meeting. The chapter to come is the one where the two programs play each other as competitive peers for the first time in nearly a decade, with the Big Ten's structural elevation in the conference's back pocket and Notre Dame's CFP-level institutional status as the program's defining identity. November is six months away. We will be there.
