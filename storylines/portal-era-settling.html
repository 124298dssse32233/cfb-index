Spring 2026: Portal-Evaluation as a Coaching Specialty, the Receivers Gold Rush, and the Next Cignettis

Four years deep into the portal era. The chaos hypothesis is dead. The architecture practitioners have been identified. The next coaching specialty is portal-evaluation: the work of figuring out which players will translate.

Builds on prior chapters
CH 01April 2023: Two Windows In, and the Chaos Hypothesis Is About to Be Tested by Data
CH 02January 2024: The Cycle's Headline Moves and the Program Most Quietly Mastering the Architecture
CH 03January 2025: Cam Ward at Miami, Will Howard at Ohio State, and the Cignetti Indiana Cycle as Validation

The spring of 2026 finds the portal era in the structural condition we have been tracking through three previous chapters: the chaos hypothesis is dead, the program-building portal practitioners have been identified by the data, and the next cycle of professional differentiation is starting to take shape around what the more-recent chapters have begun to call portal-evaluation — the work of figuring out, in the limited window between when a player enters the portal and when the program needs to commit a roster spot, which players will translate to the new program and which will not. We picked this up in Chapter 1 by laying out the chaos-vs-structure divide. We picked it up in Chapter 2 by identifying Cignetti at Indiana as the program-building chapter the cycle had not been adequately covering. We picked it up in Chapter 3 by tracking the validation cycle that the 2024 season produced. The 2026 spring chapter is about what comes next.

The portal-evaluation specialty is the chapter that the post-Cignetti cycle has produced. Cignetti's success at Indiana, which we wrote up in Chapter 3 as a demonstration rather than a template, has produced a wave of imitation attempts at the second-tier P4 and high-end G5 levels. Some of those attempts have worked. Most of them have not. The reason most have not, by Pete Nakos's read in his March 2026 column, is that "the imitators have copied Cignetti's tactic — bringing a portal cohort from a previous program — without copying Cignetti's institutional history that made the cohort movable. The cohort approach requires multi-year relationship-building between the coach and the players. Coaches without that history have tried to assemble cohorts through the open portal market and have, predictably, gotten the worse version of cohort dynamics: players moving for money rather than for system-fit."

“The portal era is no longer settling. The portal era has settled.”

— Pete Nakos, On3 March imitators column

The portal-evaluation work — the structural skill that the post-Cignetti cycle has revealed as the next coaching specialty — is what the architects who have not had multi-year FCS relationships have had to build instead. The work involves: high-quality position-coach scouting of portal candidates, structured interviews that test for cultural fit before the financial deal closes, NIL-collective coordination that does not over-pay for players whose translation is uncertain, and an institutional patience that allows the program to walk away from a player who is not the right fit even when the open-market valuation suggests otherwise. The programs that have built this work most-effectively, by the 2026 cycle's data, include:

Drinkwitz at Auburn, in his first cycle. We tracked this in the Coaching Carousel thread; the portal moves Drinkwitz made were targeted, position-of-need, and reportedly involved structured cultural-fit evaluations that exceeded most P4 programs' processes.

Mike Elko at Texas A&M, in his second cycle. Elko's portal additions in 2025 produced the program's first 10-win season in eight years; his 2026 portal cycle, smaller and more-targeted than 2025's, suggests the same evaluation discipline.

Brent Pry at Virginia Tech, who has been quietly building portal-evaluation infrastructure for three years and whose 2025 season's improvement (8-4 from 2024's 6-6) is the data point that the work is producing.

Lance Leipold at Kansas, whose smaller-portal cycles (Leipold has consistently taken 6-8 portal players per cycle, well below the P4 average of 12-15) reflect a discipline most P4 programs have not been willing to operate with.

The receivers gold rush — and this is the structural sub-story we want to dwell on, because it is the most-current chapter of the portal era's market segmentation — has been the most-active position-specific portal market of the past two years. The reasons are structural: NIL has rewarded receivers more aggressively than any other position because the NFL Draft economics for receivers have been excellent for two consecutive cycles, the spread-and-RPO offensive systems that dominate college football need three-deep receiver rooms to operate optimally, and the position's translation between programs is more reliable than any other skill position's translation. "Receivers are the most-portable players in the portal," Pete Nakos wrote in his February 2026 receivers-gold-rush analysis. "A receiver who produced at the FBS level will, with high probability, produce at his next FBS level. The translation rate for receivers in our database is approximately 78%, compared to 61% for QBs, 55% for RBs, and 45% for OL. The market has noticed. The valuations have followed."

The valuations following has produced, in the spring 2026 portal cycle, the position's first wave of $1M-plus single-cycle NIL deals for receivers who were second-tier P4 starters at their previous programs. The boards have processed this with the kind of measured anxiety that NIL valuations always produce. "We are paying receivers like first-round NFL picks before they have proven they translate to the next level," one Cover 3 listener wrote in to the show in March. "That is going to age like NFL contracts that signed Tyreek Hill before he had proven anything. Some of these moves will work. Half of them won't. The boards in the programs that overpaid will be the loudest in 2026."

What we are tracking, going forward, is the post-Cignetti portal-architecture cycle, the receivers-gold-rush market correction (which most analysts expect within 18 months), the emergence of portal-evaluation as a coaching specialty separate from portal-architecture, and the institutional adjustments programs will continue to make as the portal era settles into its mature phase. The chaos hypothesis we wrote up in Chapter 1 is dead. The validation cycle we wrote up in Chapter 3 is complete. The next cycle is the cycle of professional differentiation, in which the programs that have built the right institutional infrastructure pull away from the programs that have not.

The portal era is no longer settling. The portal era has settled. What we are tracking now is what the settled portal era looks like as a structural condition of the sport — and what the next decade of program-building looks like with the portal as a permanent feature rather than a destabilizing one. The work is the work. The chapters continue. We pick this up after the next portal cycle, when the data on receivers, on portal-evaluation discipline, and on the next Cignetti-style breakthrough will produce its own structural lessons.
