Spring 2026: The ACC Settles, Yormark Holds, and Notre Dame Stays Independent for Now

Three realignment chapters compounding. The ACC's lawsuit settled in March. The Big 12's PE deal closed in February. Notre Dame extended its independence agreement with NBC. The endgame is no longer a singular event; it is a steady-state condition.

CH 03The Middle Game: ACC Lawsuits, the SEC Whispers, and the Yormark Surprise

The realignment story has, in the spring of 2026, reached the strange place where its central uncertainties have been replaced by structural facts that nobody is fully comfortable calling "settled." The ACC's lawsuit with Florida State settled in March on terms that ESPN's Brett McMurphy summarized as "a partial weakening of the Grant of Rights, a meaningful redistribution of revenue toward FSU and Clemson, and an explicit acknowledgment that the conference's existing structure cannot survive another decade without further modification." That is not the dissolution outcome the SEC and Big Ten had been modeling. It is, plausibly, a slower path to the same destination. We picked this thread up in Chapter 3 by noting that the conferences had done preparatory work for an ACC dissolution scenario; what they did not do work for, because the work had not been visible from the outside, was the slower-decay scenario the lawsuit's settlement actually produced.

What this means in practice: FSU and Clemson are not leaving the ACC in 2026. They are not leaving in 2027. The settlement gives both programs a structurally larger share of conference revenue, a clearer path to a future opt-out (the GOR is now enforceable only through 2030, not 2036), and a contractual mechanism for renegotiation if conference revenue trails the SEC and Big Ten by more than a defined threshold. Both programs gain optionality. Both programs lose the certainty of a near-term move. The ACC, meanwhile, has bought four years of stability at a cost that will reshape its internal economics. "The settlement is the worst possible outcome for everyone," one ACC AD told Bruce Feldman last week, on background, "and it is the most likely possible outcome we were ever going to get to. The conferences that bought it can live with it. They cannot live with it forever."

The Big 12's CVC Capital deal closed in February 2026, on roughly the terms Yormark had floated in 2025. The conference's per-school distributions are now projected to land within 8% of the ACC's, with the PE infusion specifically earmarked for facilities investment, NIL collective support, and conference-level branding work. As we noted in Chapter 3, the boards split on this; the optimistic read has been broadly vindicated by the early implementation. The pessimistic read — that PE money carries strings that will manifest in a few years — has not been disproven, but it has also not been demonstrated. "We bought time," Yormark told The Athletic's Sam Khan Jr. in February. "We bought it on terms we can defend. The next conversation about the Big 12's future will happen on different ground than the last one did, because of this."

Notre Dame, for its part, extended its independent-football arrangement with NBC through 2032 in late January. The extension was not a surprise — the financial logic for independence has not fundamentally changed — but the terms of the extension were notable. NBC's annual rights payment to ND was reportedly increased by approximately 18%, with explicit language allowing the program to participate more flexibly in conference scheduling alliances during the contract term. The most-cited reading of the language is the Pete Sampson read on Locked On Notre Dame (ep 488, February 14): "The university has reaffirmed its independence and has also given itself a mechanism to revisit the question, on its own timeline, in the back half of the contract. That is the most economically rational thing the university could have done."

The boards in South Bend reacted with a relief that was, in places, almost grateful. A thread on BlueGrayGold called "Independence: extended" by GoldRush89 ran 480 replies and the dominant tone was a reassertion of the program's distinct identity against a realignment cycle that has consumed nearly every other historic independent. "We watched Penn State stop being independent in '93," one reply read. "We watched Miami stop in '91. We watched BYU stop in '23. We are still independent. We are the last one standing in the way the program has always told itself it should be standing. That matters more than the math."

What we picked up in Chapter 1 — that the Pac-12's collapse was the first dramatic instance of a process that would keep producing chapters — has held. The chapters have kept landing. The pace has slowed. The drama has migrated from press conferences to court filings to PE-deal closings to NBC contract extensions, and the drama in those venues is harder to write about than the drama in press conferences was. But the underlying process is unchanged: the consolidation of the sport's economics into two superconferences continues, and the institutions that fall outside the two-conference center continue to negotiate the terms on which they exist alongside it.

What has changed, two cycles after the Pac-12's collapse, is the timeline. The endgame is no longer a singular event. It is a steady-state condition. The ACC has bought four years. The Big 12 has bought a generation. The Pac-12-rebuild is in its third year and is still figuring itself out. Notre Dame has bought another six years of independence. The next round of meaningful structural change will land sometime between 2028 and 2032, depending on which conference's media deal expires first and which conference's discontent reaches political mass first.

This thread is going to keep updating. The next chapter is probably the SEC's response to the ACC settlement — there are whispers, sourced in a Brett McMurphy column from April 14, that the conference is exploring a "premium membership" structure that would reward the four or five highest-revenue programs differently from the rest. If those whispers harden into negotiation, that becomes Chapter 5. We will pick this up when it does.

In the meantime: the realignment endgame continues. It is no longer dramatic. It is no longer settled either.
