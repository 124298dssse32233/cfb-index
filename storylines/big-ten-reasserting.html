Spring 2026: Three's the Question, and the Sherrone Moore Year

Two cycles after Michigan's title, the conference is structurally elevated and now confronting the chapter every elevated conference eventually confronts: the third trophy is the proof.

Builds on prior chapters
CH 01January 8: Michigan Wins, and the Conference Stops Apologizing
CH 02August: The Coast-to-Coast Conference, and the Roster That Won the Argument
CH 03January: Ohio State Wins, and the Conference Has Two in a Row

The spring of 2026 finds the Big Ten in the position the SEC was in around 2008 — a conference with two consecutive national titles, a roster of contenders that is now broader than at any point in its modern history, and the structural question of whether the elevation will produce a run or peak as a back-to-back. The third trophy is the proof. We picked this up in Chapter 1 by noting that one trophy gave the conference permission to stop arguing from a defensive position; in Chapter 2 by tracking the expansion absorption that gave the conference its widest geographic footprint in history; in Chapter 3 by noting that back-to-back was structurally different from one trophy. The third would be different again.

The 2025 season did not produce the third. Texas won the College Football Playoff. Texas is in the SEC. The argument that two trophies for the Big Ten was the start of a run has been complicated by the fact that the conference did not win the third in the immediate next opportunity. The argument that two trophies was a peak has been complicated by the fact that the conference's losses in 2025 were close, structural-quality losses (Ohio State lost in the CFP semifinal to Texas in a game that was tied at the half) rather than the kind of blowout exits that signal a conference's competitive ceiling. The truth is between the two arguments. The conference is structurally elevated. The conference is also not yet a run. The third trophy is what it always was: the marker.

“Two cycles is a feature. Five is a fixture. The conference is in year three of asking whether it can be a fixture.”

— Pete Sampson, The Athletic Moore-year evaluation

The Sherrone Moore year — Michigan's second under Moore, which finished 9-4 with a Citrus Bowl appearance and a 2025 season that saw the program continue to rebuild from the personnel losses that followed the title — is the chapter we should look at hardest. Moore inherited the trophy from Harbaugh, as we noted in Chapter 1. His first year (8-5) was the price of replacement and roster turnover. His second year was the proof-of-concept year. The proof of concept was incomplete. Michigan beat Penn State, beat USC, lost to Ohio State (predictably) and to Iowa (less predictably) and to Oregon (in the conference's most-watched game of the season). The Citrus Bowl, against an SEC opponent (Tennessee), Michigan won. The bowl win does not produce a championship. The bowl win does produce evidence that the program is now functioning, on its own terms, under a coach who is not the coach who built the trophy.

Pete Sampson at The Athletic wrote the most-careful evaluation of Moore's second year on January 4. "Sherrone Moore has done what the program needed him to do in the immediate post-Harbaugh period," Sampson wrote. "He has not done it at championship pace. He has done it at the pace of a coach building a program after a once-in-a-generation title cycle ended with the head coach leaving for the NFL. The work is incremental. The work is real. The trophy from January 2024 will not be repeated by Sherrone Moore in his first three years; the program is not built that way under his leadership. The program may produce another trophy in his fifth or sixth year. The program will not produce one in his second." That is the most-honest read available. The MGoBlog boards have, broadly, accepted it. The TheVictorVictorius reply we cited in Chapter 1 about whether the architect's house is now Moore's house has aged into a chapter of its own. The house is Moore's. The house is standing. The house is not, currently, producing trophies. The house is producing 9-4 seasons that are 9-4 because the conference around Michigan is structurally better than the conference Michigan won the trophy in.

What the structurally-better-conference around Michigan looks like — and what the boards are processing in the spring — is the cleanest evidence that Chapter 2's "the conference now matches the trophy" framing has held. Ohio State was 11-2 in 2025 and made the CFP semifinal. Penn State was 11-2 and made the bracket as a 4-seed; Drew Allar's senior year delivered exactly what the program had been waiting for, and the program lost in the CFP quarterfinal in a way that, in 2008, would have been described as predictable disappointment and now reads as evidence-of-real-elevation that the program has not converted. Oregon went 12-1 in its second conference season and won the Big Ten Championship Game, beating Ohio State in a game that defenders of the conference's elevation will quote for a decade. USC under Lincoln Riley went 9-4, played a defense that Riley re-staffed for the 2025 season, and finally looked like a program that might compete for a Big Ten title in 2026 or 2027. Indiana, after Curt Cignetti's overperforming '24 season, regressed to 8-5 — predictable, Cignetti has been promoted to the SEC vacancy at Auburn (which we will track in the carousel thread), and the Hoosiers are now searching for a successor who can sustain the elevation Cignetti built.

The recruiting evidence has continued to bend toward the conference's structural reassertion. The 2026 cycle's December early signing period — which is the cycle that reflects 24 months of trophy-driven recruiting work — saw the Big Ten close the per-recruit gap to within 0.04 of the SEC, the smallest gap in the modern era of the major-services scale. Ohio State signed the No. 1 class. Penn State signed the No. 5 class. Oregon signed the No. 8 class. The conference's top three classes were better, on average, than the SEC's top three. The next three were not. The structural reassertion is real at the top. The depth comparison continues.

The Solid Verbal pod's spring update (ep 612, March 18) made the framing that we think will hold for the next chapter. "The Big Ten is the conference of the moment," Mark Rogers said. "The Big Ten is also a conference whose 'moment' has now lasted two cycles and is asking whether it can last five. Two cycles is a feature. Five is a fixture. The conference is in year three of asking whether it can be a fixture. The answer to that question is the next several years' worth of trophies. The 2026 season is the next data point."

We pick this up after the 2026 season's verdict lands. The chapter to come is the one where Day, Moore, Lanning at Oregon, Franklin at Penn State, and Riley at USC all arrive at the same stretch of November having had the off-season to prove what the conference is. The conference, two cycles in, is what we said it was: structurally elevated, not yet a fixture, holding the trophies it has and asking whether it can hold the next one.
