Two Cycles In: The November That No Longer Eliminates

After two seasons of the 12-team bracket, the regular season has settled into a new rhythm — and the part the architects didn't fully predict is what the rhythm has done to October.

Builds on prior chapters

CH 02The First Field, and the Argument That Was Supposed to End
CH 03After the Trophy: What the Bracket Actually Produced

We are 16 months past the first 12-team field and eight months past the second one, and the early read on the format is this: the regular season has not died, as the pessimists feared, but it has changed shape. The November that used to eliminate now seeds. The October that used to be a tune-up has quietly become the most consequential month on the calendar. And the place where the format has produced the cleanest dividends — the place even the format's critics now grant something to — is the bottom half of the bracket.

The October-as-the-new-November read is the one we want to defend with evidence. In Chapter 3 we noted that Ohio State, the eventual champion, lost in November and used the bracket to relitigate the loss across three road games. What we did not yet have was the second-cycle data. The second cycle confirms the pattern. Texas, the 2025-season champion, lost their lone regular-season game in early October to Georgia. They did not lose again. They were a five seed because of that one loss. They won the bracket. They did not win the SEC. They won the format that the SEC's loudest voices spent eighteen months arguing the format would devalue them within.

“We used to play to win in November. We now play to lock by November. There's a difference.”

— Cover 3, Episode 412 (Pete Thamel)

This is the surprising piece. The format's critics — Forde most consistently, Finebaum at every available volume — have spent two cycles arguing that the bracket's expansion would weaken the regular season. The two-cycle evidence says: the regular season's Saturdays in November have weakened, but the regular season's Saturdays in October have strengthened. October has become the month where seeding gets locked. October losses now matter more, not less, because they're the losses you can't recover from in time to climb back to a top-four seed and a bye. November losses you can absorb and still get a bye if you started the month at 8-0. October losses, if you started the month at 4-0 and lose your headline game, knock you down a tier the bracket then makes very hard to climb back from.

Pete Thamel made this case on the Cover 3 pod two weeks ago (ep 412, April 9): "The new emotional center of the regular season is the Red River game, the Iron Bowl-in-its-late-October-window, the Michigan-Ohio State, the Oregon-Washington — the games that historically drove November now drive October because they ended up scheduled in the second weekend of October in a few of these conferences and because their losers can no longer count on running the table to climb back. The bye is the prize. The bye gets locked by mid-November. The work to lock it gets done in October."

The boards have, as ever, gotten there before the columns. A long thread on 11Warriors in late October 2025 by user TressBall titled "October is the new November" ran 540 replies and worked through every Big Ten contender's path. The dominant conclusion was that the conference had structurally devalued its November slate by front-loading marquee matchups in the schedule's middle. "We used to play to win in November," one reply read. "We now play to lock by November. There's a difference. The Big Ten is going to have to think about whether the difference is actually what it wanted."

The bottom-of-the-bracket dividend, meanwhile, is the place where even the critics are starting to give the format credit. As we noted in Chapter 1 — the Saturdays nobody used to talk about — the Group of Five champion's path now matters in ways it didn't before. Two consecutive G5 champs (Boise State '24, Tulane '25) made the bracket. Both lost in the first round, but the wider story is the one Andy Staples flagged in his On3 column last month: "The G5 final-month conference races are the only races in the country whose stakes have been clearly improved by the format. A 9-3 Mountain West co-leader has more national stakes in November than they have ever had. The auto-bid did that. The format did that." The G5 ranks are not pretending the bracket has equalized them. They are noting that the bracket has surfaced them.

What hasn't worked, two cycles in: the bowl middle, as we flagged in Chapter 3, is a problem and the bowl middle's problem is getting worse. The Citrus Bowl drew its lowest viewership in 14 years this past December. The Outback drew a number it hasn't drawn since the early 2000s. The Sugar and Orange (still quarterfinal hosts) printed money. The CFP earnings have grown roughly 38% under the new format; bowl-pool earnings (ex-CFP rounds) are down 21%. The athletic directors at programs whose bowl tie-ins land them in the middle pool are quietly furious. The next collective bargain on bowl rights, due in 2027, is going to be ugly.

What the second-cycle verdict on the format actually is: a tournament that produces better football, a regular season that has rebalanced toward October without losing its season-long structure, a bowl ecosystem that the format is materially weakening, and a conversation around the format that — in TideRollerSC's TideFans line from last week — "is finally starting to ask the questions that two seasons in look like the right questions, even if they're harder to answer than the first-cycle questions were."

This thread continues. Chapter 5 picks up after the spring meetings, when the conferences are scheduled to formally raise the bowl-revenue question for the first time. Whether they raise it loud enough to matter is the next thing we will be tracking.
