# CFB Fan Delight And Data-Viz Brief

Research date: 2026-04-21

## Audience

The target audience is:

- casual enough to want guidance
- nerdy enough to love argument fuel
- emotionally invested enough to care about momentum, panic, disrespect, and hype

That audience does **not** want a sterile quant terminal.

They want:

- a fast read
- a strong point of view
- a couple of numbers they can argue with
- visuals that make the story obvious

## Product thesis

The most delightful version of this feature is not `sentiment analytics`.

It is:

- `How the fanbase feels`
- `How the rest of college football feels`
- `What the market is doing`
- `Where those things disagree`

The winning emotional loop is:

1. `show me my team's vibe`
2. `show me whether outsiders respect us`
3. `show me whether the market agrees`
4. `show me why it changed`

## The best narrative angles

These are the angles most likely to feel fresh and fan-friendly.

### 1. Overreaction Watch

Best question:

- `did the market move more than the public mood, or did the public mood move more than the market?`

Why it works:

- fans instantly understand it
- it creates weekly argument fuel
- it rewards disagreement instead of hiding it

### 2. Respect Gap

Best question:

- `are fans more bullish than the national conversation?`

Why it works:

- every fanbase loves feeling overlooked or validated
- it gives a strong identity narrative

### 3. Panic Meter

Best question:

- `how fast did confidence collapse after a loss, injury, or ugly win?`

Why it works:

- emotional volatility is fun
- it naturally fits sports fandom

### 4. Rival Heat

Best question:

- `who is getting mocked, feared, or obsessed over by rivals?`

Why it works:

- rivalry emotion is part of the entertainment
- this gives the site personality

### 5. Market Versus Model Versus Mood

Best question:

- `what does the model say, what does the market say, and what are people saying?`

Why it works:

- three-way disagreement is richer than a single number
- it creates an immediate story structure

## Best page modules

## Homepage

Best homepage modules:

- `Biggest Vibe Shifts This Week`
- `Market-Mood Disagreements`
- `Most Respected Teams`
- `Most Panicked Fanbases`
- `Rival Heat Leaders`

The homepage should feel like:

- an editorial front page for smart fans
- not a pile of database widgets

## Team page

Best team page modules:

- `Fan Pulse`
- `National Mood`
- `Rival Heat`
- `Vibe Over Time`
- `What Changed This Week`
- `Most Loved Players`
- `Most Criticized Players`

The first screen should answer:

- `how are we doing?`
- `how are we being talked about?`
- `who is driving the conversation?`

## Matchup page

Best matchup page modules:

- `Market line movement`
- `Public mood split`
- `Which fanbase is calmer`
- `What both sides are worried about`
- `Where the model, market, and mood disagree`

This page is where the concept becomes addictive.

## Best chart types

## 1. Annotated slope chart

Use for:

- open-to-close line movement
- week-over-week vibe change

Why it works:

- one glance explains direction and magnitude
- annotations make it editorial

## 2. Dumbbell chart

Use for:

- `Fan Pulse` versus `National Mood`
- `model probability` versus `market probability`

Why it works:

- disagreement becomes the visual, not buried text

## 3. Stacked emotion ribbon

Use for:

- hope / anger / fear / confidence over time

Why it works:

- fans think emotionally, not in raw polarity
- ribbons tell a story better than a single sentiment line

## 4. Quadrant plot

Use for:

- `market move` on one axis
- `sentiment move` on the other axis

Quadrants become instant labels:

- `everyone bought in`
- `market skeptical, fans euphoric`
- `fans panicking, market calm`
- `full collapse`

## 5. Small-multiple spark cards

Use for:

- top 10 teams or fanbases
- weekly roundups

Why it works:

- mobile-friendly
- scannable
- good for habitual visits

## Visual ideas to avoid

- giant all-in-one dashboards
- word clouds
- 3D charts
- heatmaps without a clear story
- one giant sentiment score with no explanation
- unlabeled red/green scales that assume every user thinks like a bettor

## Copy and labeling guidance

Use labels that feel like sports media, not enterprise analytics.

Good:

- `Fan Pulse`
- `National Mood`
- `Rival Heat`
- `Panic Meter`
- `Respect Gap`
- `Overreaction Watch`
- `Belief Check`

Bad:

- `sentiment classifier output`
- `social polarity index`
- `topic model cluster 3`
- `engagement-weighted opinion intensity`

## What will delight fans most

The delight comes from three things.

### 1. Explainable disagreement

Fans love being shown:

- when they disagree with outsiders
- when the market disagrees with their feelings
- when the site model disagrees with both

### 2. Emotional truth

Fans respond more to:

- `panic`
- `hope`
- `rage`
- `respect`
- `delusion`

than to generic positivity or negativity.

### 3. Strong weekly storytelling

The best version is a recurring editorial rhythm:

- `who gained belief`
- `who lost belief`
- `where the market moved too far`
- `where fanbases are lying to themselves`

That rhythm can power both the homepage and social snippets.

## Best v1 scope for delight

For v1, the most delightful feature set is:

- team-level `Fan Pulse`
- team-level `National Mood`
- team-level `Rival Heat`
- matchup-level `Market vs Mood`
- weekly `Biggest Vibe Shifts`

That is enough to feel distinctive without pretending we built a full social-intelligence empire.

## Best later extensions

- player-level approval and blame
- awards buzz trackers
- coaching hot-seat mood
- transfer-portal belief swings
- playoff futures mood versus market

## Design principle

Every chart should answer one sentence-level question.

If the chart cannot be summarized in one sentence, it is probably too complicated for this audience.
