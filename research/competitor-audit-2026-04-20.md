# College Football Site Competitor Audit

Date: April 20, 2026

This audit focuses on live products that shape user expectations for college-football rankings, predictions, and analytics.

## Benchmarks reviewed

- [ESPN FPI](https://www.espn.com/college-football/fpi)
- [TeamRankings College Football](https://www.teamrankings.com/college-football/ranking/predictive-by-other)
- [Game on Paper CFB](https://gameonpaper.com/CFB/)
- [CFB Ranking Calculator](https://www.cfb-ranking.com/)
- [CFB HUB / Terranalytics](https://cfb.terranalytics.com/)

## What the best current sites do well

### 1. They make the first screen immediately useful

- ESPN FPI makes the value proposition obvious right away: one screen with `FPI`, `Resume`, and `Efficiencies`, plus season and conference filters.
- TeamRankings makes the main jobs obvious: rankings, matchups, schedules, projections, standings, and stats.
- CFB Ranking Calculator explains the interaction quickly: rank teams, compare resumes, and test your biases.

Takeaway:

- elite sports products reduce “what am I supposed to do here?” friction on the first screen
- they present rankings as one part of a broader workflow, not the whole product

### 2. They let users move from board view to argument view

- ESPN supports quick switching between strength, resume, and efficiency lenses.
- TeamRankings pushes users from rankings into matchup pages, schedules, odds context, and standings projections.
- CFB Ranking Calculator is built around direct comparison and debate, not just passive browsing.

Takeaway:

- strong users want to answer questions, not just read a list
- comparison, matchup, and “why is Team A above Team B?” tooling is core product value

### 3. They feel alive because they are organized around time

- ESPN and TeamRankings both let users move by season and by current board state quickly.
- Game on Paper is heavily game-first and preview-first, so it feels attached to the live weekly calendar.
- Terranalytics leans into “dashboard” thinking with rivalry spotlights, portal pulse, recruiting watch, and schedule/results replay.

Takeaway:

- the strongest products feel like a weekly habit
- archive, preview, and change-over-time surfaces matter almost as much as the current top 25

### 4. They make users trust the numbers with context

- ESPN separates `FPI`, `Resume`, and `Efficiencies`, which gives users a mental model.
- Game on Paper is known for game-level analytical framing rather than just a final rating output.
- TeamRankings surrounds predictions with many adjacent tables, which signals depth even when the UI is utilitarian.

Takeaway:

- trust comes from exposing multiple views of the same team
- users accept black-box models more readily when the surrounding product provides evidence and explainability

## Where our site is now strong

- the all-level concept is more ambitious than the mainstream products above
- the team pages already do a better job than many sites at showing rating movement and season continuity
- the homepage now has expandable rows, conference pages, matchup tooling, and a weekly archive
- the site has a cleaner editorial feel than spreadsheet-style analytics pages

## Where our site is still behind best-in-class products

### 1. Game pages are still the biggest missing surface

The best football products are not only team-first. They are also game-first.

We still need:

- dedicated game preview pages
- dedicated game recap pages
- projected score, spread, and key matchup breakdowns at the game level
- game-level play-by-play or drive story surfaces where data allows

### 2. We do not yet have a real compare-teams debate tool

We now have matchup projections, but that is not the same as a true comparison product.

We still need:

- side-by-side team compare pages
- resume compare pages
- “why Team A ranks above Team B” factor breakdowns
- historical head-to-head and common-opponent context

### 3. Recruiting, transfers, and roster continuity are not yet productized

Terranalytics-style preseason and roster surfaces are now table stakes for serious fan-built CFB products.

We still need:

- roster turnover dashboards
- transfer portal impact pages
- recruiting class strength surfaces
- returning production and quarterback continuity modules
- preseason priors pages that explain why teams begin where they do

### 4. We still need a stronger calendar experience

The site is better now that it has a weekly archive, but the next leap is a real weekly operating system for fans.

We still need:

- weekly watchlist pages
- “best games this week” surfaces
- upset watch and volatility watch
- playoff race pages
- bowl / conference championship / playoff bracket tracking

### 5. We need richer visual storytelling on the home page

The current home page is much stronger than before, but the best products still do a better job of making the page feel alive at a glance.

We still need:

- hero-level weekly story modules
- larger editorial callouts around the most important teams and games
- better visual hierarchy between the ranking board and the surrounding story cards
- stronger logo / helmet / venue artwork integration where SportsDB can help

## Best next product steps

### Highest priority

1. Build real game preview pages and recap pages.
2. Build team-vs-team compare pages with resume and power breakdowns.
3. Turn recruiting, transfers, and returning production into first-class preseason surfaces.

### Medium priority

1. Add weekly watchlist, upset watch, and playoff race pages.
2. Build richer historical season pages once more years are backfilled.
3. Add better artwork and brand assets through SportsDB enrichment.

### Lower priority

1. Add maps and geography layers.
2. Add user-personalized ranking weights.
3. Add editorial blog or notebook pages tied to weekly model changes.

## Product principle going forward

If we want this to feel like the best college-football site on the internet, the site cannot be “just rankings plus team pages.”

It needs to become a full CFB operating system with five entry points:

1. Rankings
2. Matchups
3. Teams
4. Games
5. Time

If we win those five surfaces, the all-level model becomes the differentiator instead of just a novelty.
